# Domain Analysis

## Purpose
Use this document to explore and clarify the problem domain before requirements, architecture, or implementation decisions are made.

This document should capture domain knowledge, language, responsibilities, processes, rules, constraints, and unresolved questions without defining product scope, technical design, database schema, or implementation tasks.

## Terminology
List important domain terms, alternate names, ambiguous words, and short neutral definitions that help stakeholders and developers build shared understanding.

## Stakeholders
Identify people, groups, organizations, or external parties who participate in or are affected by the domain.

## Roles and Responsibilities
Describe domain roles and the responsibilities they hold in real-world processes, independent of application permissions or user-interface design.

## Knowledge Maintained by the System
Capture information the future system may need to know, remember, organize, or make available in order to support the domain.

Keep this section conceptual; do not turn it into a database schema, entity model, or implementation design.

## Business Processes
Describe real-world processes, activities, handoffs, inputs, outputs, timing, and recurring patterns that shape the domain.

## Decision Making
Record decisions made within the domain, who is responsible for them, what information supports them, and what consequences they have.

## Business Rules
Capture policies, invariants, eligibility criteria, validations, obligations, and exceptions that are meaningful in the domain.

## Constraints
Document domain constraints such as organizational, legal, operational, timing, communication, legacy, or external limitations.

Avoid mixing domain constraints with chosen technical constraints unless the technical constraint already exists independently of the new system.

## Open Questions
Track unresolved questions, assumptions that need validation, areas of disagreement, and topics that require stakeholder input.
